Food Access Across the US

Yulim Kim and Mikaela Sherry

A significant barrier in food accessibility is the lack of a vehicle when the supermarket is far away. The USDA definition of a food desert is “A tract with at least 500 people, or 33 percent of the population, living more than one-half mile (urban areas) or 10 miles (rural areas) from the nearest supermarket, supercenter, or large grocery store.” Being far from a grocery store can inhibit one's ability to obtain fresh and healthy food, and not having access to a vehicle will further exacerbate this issue. Having access to a vehicle is typically an indicator within socio-economics and can represent a form of independence that not all have access to. We aim to create a dashboard that will help stakeholders to identify counties that are having issues with accessibility to supermarkets because they do not have access to a vehicle.

This tool allows stakeholders to investigate food inequity throughout the United States. It uses data from a 2019 dataset from the US Department of Agriculture. The dataset shows whether a household has access to a vehicle at various distances from a supermarket - 1/2 mile, 1 mile, 10 miles, and 20 miles. Researchers can use this tool to identify counties that need food assistance across the US.
